Overview

Planning and learning are two crucial capabilities of robots. While planning seeks to solve a task by synthesizing policies on the fly, learning seeks to obtain solutions from experiences provided by data. The two domains have independently advanced in the past decades and both achieved remarkable success on their most suitable tasks. There is a noticeable trend to combine these two capabilities. Impressive success has been demonstrated by AlphaZero and MuZero, showing that the integration of planning and learning can advance human-level intelligence. Particularly, there are two complementary directions: learning to plan and planning to learn. From the perspective of planning, there are important attempts seeking to learn sampling distributions, action abstractions, heuristics, and representations for planning. From the perspective of learning, there are prominent efforts to enable active and uncertainty-aware learning, and to incorporate existing models to augment learning and achieve sample-efficiency. However, what is missing is a systematic discussion on where and how these two strategies shall be best applied and how they can be seamlessly integrated.

In this workshop, we aim to bring together researchers in the planning and learning sub-domains, to discuss opportunities to integrate them — by planning to learn and learning to plan— and to incubate the next level of intelligence. Particularly, we will discuss what and how to learn for planning, what and how to plan for learning, as well as principled choices and combinations between these two.

Discussions

Panel 1 - What, where, and how:

  • What: what to plan for and what to learn for?
  • Where: when do we want to integrate planning and learning? where to use planning as the main tool, and where to use learning as the main tool?
  • How: how are the principles of planning to learn and learning to plan different from standard planning and learning principles?
  • How to do it “properly”: what theoretical properties remain important for the integrated systems? How do we achieve them? Are new forms of theoretical guarantees necessary?
  • Other concerns: safety, computational cost, interpretability, etc.

Panel 2 - Retrospects and prospects:

  • From decomposition to integration: what are the key improvements?
  • Lessons learned from the past: what is proven to be right/promising, what has been proven to be wrong/not-so-promising?
  • What have we achieved till now by integrating planning and learning? What is still lacking?
  • With recent developments of planning and learning sub-domains, what are the new chances for planning to learn, learning to plan, and their combinations?
  • Connection with relevant schemes, e.g, learning to learn (meta-learning), planning to plan (TAMP), active learning, etc.

Call for Contributions

We invite participants from all fields of robotics to submit extended abstracts related to the workshop topic, anywhere on the spectrum between model-based planning and model-free learning. Submissions should be up to 2-4 pages + references using the RSS template and should not be anonymized.

Submissions can include original research, late-breaking results, position papers, and literature reviews. Significant overlap with work submitted to other venues is acceptable, as long as it has not yet been published or presented.

Accepted submissions will be presented in 2-minute spotlight talks and in a dedicated poster session. All accepted abstracts will be posted on the workshop website. The talks will be livestreamed and recorded.

Organizers

  • Panpan Cai

    National University of Singapore (NUS)

    Panpan CAI is a postdoctoral research fellow at the National University of Singapore, working with David Hsu on integrated planning and learning, planning under uncertainty, and their applications on autonomous driving in crowded environments. She received her Ph.D. with Cai Yiyu and Zheng Jianmin at Nanyang Technological University. Panpan’s vision is to develop robots that can combine reasoning and learning to seamlessly interact with large-scale, dynamic, and interactive environments and accomplish challenging tasks.

  • Peter Karkus

    National University of Singapore (NUS)

    Peter Karkus is a PhD candidate at the National University of Singapore advised by Prof. David Hsu. He has previously spent time at MIT as a visiting researcher and at Google and DeepMind as a research intern. Peter's vision is to build human-level robot intelligence by combining structure and reasoning with learning. Peter's recent works are on neural networks that encode differentiable robot algorithms in order to learn partially observable planning, visual navigation, mapping and localization tasks. His work has been recognized by a number of awards, including a Fulbright scholarship, NUS research achievement awards, and best paper nominations.

  • Gilwoo Lee

    Gilwoo Lee is the founder CEO of Zordi, Inc., an agricultural startup focusing on controlled environment agriculture. Lee has received her Ph.D. degree from the University of Washington, where she was advised by Siddhartha S. Srinivasa. She has worked on Bayesian Reinforcement Learning, learning and planning under uncertainty, and human-robot interaction, with applications ranging from assistive robots to mobile racecars.

  • Yiyuan Lee
